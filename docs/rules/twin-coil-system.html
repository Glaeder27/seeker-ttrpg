4.3 ‚Ä¢ How Many Risk Dice Should the GM Use?

When deciding how many to assign to a roll, the GM should weigh both narrative logic and mechanical balance. Risk isn't just about how hard something is ‚Äî it's about how badly it could go wrong, and how likely that is within the fiction.

Key Variables to Evaluate

To determine the appropriate Risk level, the GM should consider the context of the action from multiple angles. The following are common factors that often contribute to narrative danger and mechanical risk:

  • Environmental Danger: Is the character acting in unstable, hazardous, or treacherous terrain? Scaling a rain-slick cliff or operating machinery during a storm naturally invites more .
  • Attention and Exposure: Is the action likely to attract unwanted notice? Picking a lock in a quiet hallway may be low risk ‚Äî but doing it under torchlight with guards nearby increases the danger.
  • Complexity and Precision: Does the action require fine control, delicate timing, or multi-step execution? The more room there is for error, the more are appropriate.
  • Supernatural Interference: Does the magic or creature involved have unpredictable or corruptive qualities? reflect the instability of arcane energies, curses, or unknowable forces.
  • Speed or Desperation: Is the character rushing or under pressure? Actions taken in haste or under duress are more likely to misfire or cause unintended consequences.

Extensions ‚∏∫ ‚ú¶ ‚∏∫

The following optional rules expand the Twin Coil System, offering greater narrative granularity, dramatic tension, or strategic depth. Use them individually or mix and match to suit your table‚Äôs tone and playstyle.

Rule Options

1. Degrees of Success Optional

From passable to legendary.

Not all successes are equal. This variant grades how well an action succeeds based on the number of that roll :

  • 1 Success ‚Üí Basic Success
    The action succeeds ‚Äî just barely. You get what you wanted, but nothing more.
  • 2 Successes ‚Üí Strong Success
    The action is executed cleanly. You gain your goal and possibly a small edge.
  • 3+ Successes ‚Üí Critical Success
    The result exceeds expectations. You gain your goal and something extra: a bonus, an opportunity, a lasting advantage.

This adds weight to large dice pools and lets the GM reward exceptional rolls with enhanced effects or momentum.

2. Degrees of Complication Optional

When things go wrong ‚Äî how wrong?

This variant adds severity to complications based on how many roll :

  • 1 Risk Success ‚Üí Minor Complication
    A nuisance or delay. Something goes slightly awry: a noise, a lost item, increased tension.
  • 2 Risk Successes ‚Üí Serious Complication
    A meaningful setback. A wound, a cost, a tactical disadvantage.
  • 3+ Risk Successes ‚Üí Catastrophic Complication
    A turning point. A dangerous twist or a lasting consequence ‚Äî something breaks, someone bleeds, things escalate.

This deepens the drama of risky actions, making danger feel more volatile and story-driven.

3. Push Mechanic Experimental

Tempt fate. Try again.

When a character fails (or barely succeeds), they may choose to Push ‚Äî rerolling a single Action Die in exchange for a cost.

Offer one of the following trade-offs (or allow both):

  • Spend 1 Resource (Fatigue, Gear, Arcane Power, etc.)
    The character pushes themselves, consumes an item, or expends effort.
  • Add 1 Risk Die
    The character pushes recklessly, raising the stakes. Success becomes more likely ‚Äî but so does danger.

Pushing should feel risky, not routine. It gives players a dramatic choice at the edge of success.
