Section I

What is Seeker? ⸺ ✦ ⸺

Seeker is a tabletop roleplaying game of forbidden power, colonial secrets, and arcane exploration. It does not reinvent history — it reveals what was hidden beneath it. Set during the first voyages to the New World, the game chronicles a world forever altered by contact with ancient forces and unnatural phenomena.

This section introduces the essential ideas and structure of the game through a brief FAQ — a tool for new players, and a primer for what lies beyond the veil.

The following material is based on private research conducted between 2017 and 2024, drawing from restricted documents preserved within the Secret Vatican Archives. The contents — fragmented reports, coded journals, and anonymous letters — suggest the existence of early expeditions that encountered phenomena unrecorded in any official history.

For reasons of personal safety and academic integrity, I publish this under the name Alonso de Luna.

— A. de L.

Who are the Seekers?

The Seekers (Buscadores in Spanish) are the player characters — field agents operating under a covert mandate to investigate unexplained phenomena, retrieve arcane relics, and neutralize emerging threats tied to the expansion into the New World.

Officially, they do not exist.

The order of the Seekers was founded in secret in the winter of 1491, as a clandestine alliance between Queen Isabella of Castile and Henry VII of England. Alarmed by early reports from explorers and missionaries — whispers of bleeding idols, unkillable beasts, and sacrificial magic — the two monarchs agreed to form a hidden commission beyond the reach of Church or Crown.

Operating under codenames and false commissions, these agents traveled alongside conquistadors, chroniclers, and traders — often disguised as naturalists, theologians, cartographers, or members of the clergy.

Some among them were names we know today: painters, inventors, navigators, and philosophers whose public lives masked their true missions. The works they left behind, such as maps, journals, altarpieces, even mechanical devices, contain traces of what they encountered. But the full truth was buried, redacted, or sealed by the founding compact.

The Seekers are the custodians of that hidden war — a war not for land, but for reality itself.

As a player, you take on the role of one such Seeker. Whether driven by faith, science, greed, guilt, or curiosity, you will confront forces that defy reason... and return changed, if you return at all.

What is the setting of Seeker?

It is 1495. The world has not merely expanded — it has ruptured.

The jungles breathe. The sea sings. Ancient temples bleed. What began as conquest has become contact with something older, deeper, and inhuman.

The story begins just three years earlier, with the first landfall of Columbus’s expedition. Though official records speak only of gold, spice, and fertile land, those who returned from the voyage whispered of stranger things. Several crewmen fell ill within weeks of disembarkation, afflicted with fevers that twisted flesh and mind alike. Some grew ravenous, mutated, unrecognizable. Their existence was denied, their remains destroyed, their names erased.

In the years that followed, new expeditions uncovered impossible sights: native civilizations that harnessed blood rituals to bend reality; cities of stone and obsidian raised without tools or scaffolding; pyramids whose construction modern scholars still cannot explain. The land itself responded with hostility reshaping, collapsing, resisting. Soil turned black. Rivers reversed. Plants fed on bone. It was as if the very earth rejected the presence of foreign bodies.

These events, swiftly concealed by the Crown of Castile, attracted the attention of powerful interests. Queen Isabella and Henry Tudor saw in them not blasphemy, but opportunity: a means to dominate the world through secret knowledge. The Vatican, however, interpreted these signs as clear evidence of infernal contamination, and moved to eradicate them at the root.

Thus began a hidden conflict: not between nations, but between visions of truth. A race for control of the arcane, of the unknown, of the soul of the New World. The age of empires was not built on spice and silver alone — it was built on secrecy, sacrifice, and the silence of those who knew too much.

What makes Seeker different from other TTRPGs?

Seeker reimagines the core of tabletop roleplaying by emphasizing narrative risk, player intent, and a world that pushes back. Its systems are tightly interwoven to support tense exploration, meaningful choices, and escalating consequences.

No Archetypes. Seeker breaks away from traditional roleplaying templates. There are no classes, races, spell slots, or fixed archetypes. Instead of locking characters into predefined paths, Seeker lets them grow dynamically across three Tracks — Combat, Exploration, and Social — by earning experience and investing it into modular Skills and Masteries. Each ability can be further modified through Augmentations, allowing players to shape truly unique characters tailored to their playstyle and story arc.

Narrative-focused Dice Engine. At the core of the system lies the Twin Coil System, a dice mechanic that resolves actions by rolling two types of dice: Action, which determine success, and Risk, which introduce complications. This framework allows for non-binary and nuanced results — not just success or failure, but also mixed outcomes like “success with complication” or “failure with benefit.” It encourages drama, tension, and consequence while minimizing math at the table. Rolls are clean, fast, and easy to interpret.

Tag System. Abilities, items, effects, and actions are all structured through a Tag system, which standardizes language and promotes synergy. Tags like Toxin, Maneuver, Strike, or Defensive define how abilities interact — skills sharing a Tag can enhance one another, trigger combo effects, or modify outcomes. This creates emergent gameplay with fewer words and clearer logic: rather than repeating complex rules, a single Tag description applies across the system.

Strategic Combat System. Combat in Seeker emphasizes tactical interplay and intent. There is no random initiative — instead, each character secretly selects a Stance at the start of the round. Stances determine initiative order, how many actions can be taken, and what kinds of Tags are allowed (e.g., Aggressive allows only Attack, Maneuver, and Strike). The system encourages coordination: characters can interact mid-action, exploit enemy vulnerabilities, and prepare combos across turns. Enemies often have multiple combat phases, requiring players to shift tactics or expose weaknesses to succeed. Defense is structured into three layers — Guard, Armor, and Health — each with its own tactical purpose and cost.

Built for Epic Stories. Beneath it all, Seeker is not just a tactical or narrative game — it is a game of tension. Whether through the Risk Dice, the Polarity mechanic (which rewards or punishes actions based on your character’s moral arc), or the design of exploration modules, the system constantly asks: how far are you willing to go?

Is there a magic system?

Not in the traditional sense. Magic in Seeker is rare, corrupting, and narrative-driven. It appears in blood rituals, cursed relics, sacred geometries, and impossible phenomena. It cannot be mastered — only endured, resisted, or unleashed.

What kind of characters can I play?

Anything from a hardened conquistador or ship surgeon, to a disillusioned priest or runaway monk, to a heretical astronomer or syncretic sorcerer. Your character is defined by their Aptitudes and the Talents they acquire through play.

What kind of gameplay should I expect?

Exploration, investigation, negotiation, and high-risk conflict. You’ll chart unknown territories, interpret omens, face spiritual afflictions, and make dangerous decisions. The game supports both tactical challenges and dramatic storytelling.

Is Seeker a horror game?

It contains horror, but is not limited to it. The tone is often uncanny, oppressive, and morally ambiguous. Fear comes not only from the unknown, but from the choices the characters must make — and what those choices cost them.

Do I need to know history to play?

No. Seeker uses history as a scaffold, not a requirement. It reimagines events through the lens of dark fantasy and myth, inviting creative freedom without demanding academic precision.

Is this game respectful of real-world cultures?

The civilizations encountered in the New World are unlike anything known to European chroniclers. Though echoes of real-world myths and cultures may be felt, these societies have no direct analogue in our own history — their mysteries remain wholly their own.
