Exploration System

b

Procedural Hex-based Exploration

Exploration in Seeker is not a passive journey, but a structured game of discovery where each step into the unknown reshapes the map. Instead of moving across a pre-drawn landscape, the Seekers reveal one hex at a time, building the New World as they advance. This procedural system ensures that no expedition is ever the same, and that each choice carries weight and consequence.

The exploration is a contest of intent between the Archivist and the Seekers. The Archivist introduces uncertainty and hardship by generating the terrain, anomalies, and dangers of each new hex. The Seekers, through their skills, masteries, and resources, attempt to mitigate risks, exploit opportunities, and transform a hostile land into one they can traverse and survive. Every revealed hex becomes a strategic battleground between adversity and adaptation.

This process balances randomness and agency. Dice rolls inject unpredictability, shaping dangers, features, and events; but the Seekers’ decisions—whether to press forward, scout, or retreat—determine how harshly those results impact their journey. The challenge is not simply to endure, but to manage resources, exploit discoveries, and anticipate what lies beyond the next edge of the map.

At its heart, exploration is a cycle of risk and reward. The Seekers must accept hardship, seize rare opportunities, and endure unexpected dangers. Boldness is rewarded, caution preserves survival, and every hex revealed is another fragment of a living world influenced by Solaria’s presence. This system transforms travel into a tense, evolving narrative where the landscape itself is both adversary and treasure.

Section II

b

Roles in Exploration

b

Division of Duties and Leadership in Exploration

Before embarking on an expedition, each Seeker must decide whether to take on a specific role within the party. These roles represent historical functions within real expeditions and grant access to Skills and Masteries that only those roles can employ. The choice of roles defines how the expedition is organized, where leadership lies, and how resources are managed during the journey.

Assignment Rules

The following rules define how roles are assigned within the group:

  • Each Seeker may take at most one role.
  • It is not mandatory to cover all available roles.
  • At least one Seeker must be assigned as Captain.

Procedure

At the start of the expedition, the party decides collectively who fills which role. This decision is locked for the duration of the journey: roles may only be reassigned once the expedition is concluded and the Seekers return to a safe haven.

Before setting out into unknown territory, the group assigns Roles. Isabel chooses Captain, Diego takes Scout, while Rodrigo assumes the role of Quartermaster.

During exploration, Diego uses his Scout Skill to automatically learn what Danger lies in the next hex. Later, Rodrigo’s Quartermaster Mastery increases the Supplies found when harvesting by +1. At the expedition’s end, Isabel’s Captain Mastery restores 3 Morale spent during the journey (equal to her Charisma score).

Roles

The following roles provide different ways of interacting with the world:

  • Captain | Capitán — The leader of the expedition. Responsible for discipline, morale, and the ultimate direction of the journey. The Captain embodies authority and vision.
  • Quartermaster | Furriel — Oversees supplies, provisions, and the fair distribution of resources. Keeps the group alive by preventing scarcity and mismanagement.
  • Scout | Explorador — Guides the party through unknown terrain, identifying dangers and paths ahead. The Scout represents keen senses and survival instincts.
  • Chaplain | Capellán Provides spiritual guidance and a moral compass. The Chaplain can inspire hope, faith, or fear, depending on the beliefs carried into the wilderness.
  • Chronicler | Cronista — Records the events of the expedition, maps lands, and preserves knowledge for posterity. Their presence ensures discoveries are not lost to memory.
  • Navigator | Navegante — Master of the stars, maps, and instruments. The Navigator ensures the group knows where it stands and how to reach its destination.
  • Artillerist | Artillero — Maintains and commands the expedition’s weapons, from arquebuses to cannons. The Artillerist embodies firepower and intimidation.
  • Interpreter | Interprete — Bridges languages and cultures, allowing parley or deception. The Interpreter is often the thin line between diplomacy and conflict.

Section III

f

Resources

f

Shared Pools and Management

Exploration is a collective endeavor. The group shares and manages key resources that measure both physical supplies and intangible resilience. Skills and Masteries can affect how efficiently these resources are spent or recovered, but their scarcity always defines the rhythm of an expedition.

Supplies

Supplies (Supplies) represent the stock of food, water, fuel, and other consumables your expedition carries. They measure the group’s ability to survive in hostile environments, sustain long journeys, and face the unknown with preparation.

How It Works

Supplies is tracked as a shared party resource. The starting value depends on your preparation, chosen Equipment, and certain Masteries. Supplies are consumed during travel, resting, or when specific abilities require expendable goods. Running out of Supplies exposes the group to hunger, thirst, fatigue, and other escalating hardships.

When It Applies

Any extended expedition, overland journey, or dangerous environment requires Supplies. They are spent daily for basic survival and may also be consumed to boost certain actions, such as treating wounds, preparing equipment, or enduring extreme conditions. If Supplies reaches 0, the group suffers penalties to Health recovery, Morale, and effectiveness in exploration.

How to Interact With It

You can restore Supplies by resupplying in settlements, scavenging in the wild, or trading with other groups. Some Skills or Masteries allow you to stretch existing stocks further, improvise substitutes, or prevent spoilage. Wise management of Supplies often means the difference between reaching your destination or collapsing along the way.

Supplies is more than just gear and rations—it’s the expedition’s lifeblood. Every meal consumed is a step deeper into the wilderness, and every shortage sharpens the tension of survival.

Morale

Morale (Morale) reflects the group’s determination, cohesion, and mental resilience in the face of hardship. It measures not only courage and loyalty, but also the trust binding Seekers together during perilous journeys.

How It Works

Morale is tracked as a shared party resource. It rises or falls depending on victories, losses, stress, and interpersonal dynamics. Certain Skills, Masteries, or Leadership actions can raise Morale, while fear, betrayal, or repeated failure may drain it.

When It Applies

Morale comes into play when facing prolonged hardship, terrifying enemies, or stressful negotiations. High Morale boosts cooperation and resistance to despair, while low values may cause hesitation, poor discipline, or outright collapse of unity.

How to Interact With It

Morale can be restored by achieving victories, sharing spoils, celebrating, or reinforcing bonds of loyalty. Characters with strong social or inspirational abilities can rally the group, while some Skills or Masteries help mitigate fear or restore confidence. Conversely, reckless actions, cruelty, or neglect can erode Morale rapidly.

Morale is the invisible thread that keeps a band of Seekers moving forward. Steel and supplies may fail—but as long as spirits endure, the expedition still has a chance.

Luck

Luck (Luck) embodies fate’s favor, uncanny coincidences, and the inexplicable survivals that often decide whether a Seeker’s story ends in glory or tragedy. It is less about skill or willpower, and more about being in the right place at the right time—or spared from disaster by chance.

How It Works

Luck is tracked as a shared expedition resource. It can be spent to avert catastrophe, bend outcomes in the Seekers’ favor, or survive events that would otherwise prove fatal. When depleted, the Archivist may draw from the Bad Luck column to intensify hazards, misfortune, or narrative twists.

When It Applies

Luck intervenes in extreme situations: sudden ambushes, deadly traps, natural disasters, or sheer chance that could make or break an expedition. It does not replace strategy or preparation, but cushions the impact of the unforeseeable.

How to Interact With It

Luck can be restored rarely—through sacred rituals, blessings, divine omens, or rare artifacts said to carry fate’s blessing. Some Masteries allow Seekers to “push” fortune in their favor, though every use risks drawing fate’s attention. Reckless overreliance on Luck can quickly drain it, leaving the group at the mercy of cruel destiny.

Luck is fickle. It may save a Seeker from a fatal blow, or doom them by absence. Wise expeditions treat Luck as the rarest resource of all.

Supplies and Attrition

When resources run out, Seekers begin to suffer direct consequences:

  • Without Supplies: Each day, every Seeker gains 1 Starvation token. Eating removes 1 token per day.
  • Without Morale: Each day, every Seeker gains 1 Despair token. Spending 1 Morale removes 1 Despair token from all.
  • Without Luck: The Archivist may apply the Bad Luck column, amplifying negative effects.

Resource Recovery

Depleted resources cannot be used until replenished. Recovery is possible through:

  • Rest: A full rest phase can restore portions of Morale or Luck.
  • Camp: Establishing a camp allows for resupply, reduced attrition, and partial recovery of resources.
  • Special Items & Abilities: Some treasures, rituals, or Masteries can instantly restore or enhance resources.

Mismanagement of resources leaves Seekers vulnerable, forcing harsher decisions and exposing them to the Archivist’s generated hazards.

a

Hex Structure

a

Pieces of a Puzzling Map

An Hex is the fundamental unit of exploration in Seeker. Each hex represents a stretch of territory large enough to contain multiple landmarks, resources, and dangers, yet small enough to be crossed within a single stage of the journey. When Seekers reveal a new hex, they are not uncovering every detail of the land, but establishing the broad strokes that define its character. These structural elements give the Archivist and the players a common framework to generate terrain, populate it with features, and shape the challenges of travel.

Hex Categories

In Seeker, explored territory is divided into two different types of Hexes, each serving a distinct role in pacing and encounter design:

  • Primary Hexes — Fully detailed; meaningful encounters or challenges.
  • Secondary Hexes — Simplified; passage or narrative pacing.

Hex Features

The following components define the identity of each hex and guide how it will impact the expedition.

Layer What it Represents
Biome The foundation of a Hex. Biomes describe the terrain and ecology—dense jungles, barren highlands, shifting dunes. Biomes dictate movement difficulty, available resources, and possible encounters. They are the canvas upon which all other features are painted.
Weather The climate within a Hex can be as dangerous as its creatures. Torrential rains flood trails, dry winds parch supplies, or sudden storms scatter parties. Weather affects travel pace, resource consumption, and can even spawn new Dangers or Anomalies.
Landmarks Visible markers within the Hex. A jagged obsidian spire, a ruined temple half-swallowed by vines, or a colossal tree can serve as orientation points, narrative anchors, or adventure hooks. Landmarks are what make each Hex distinct and memorable.
Obstacles Physical or environmental barriers that complicate progress—raging rivers, steep cliffs, labyrinthine mangroves, or cursed mists. Obstacles often require Skills, teamwork, or resource expenditure to overcome, and they slow or redirect the Seekers’ path.
Dangers Threats lurking in the Hex. These may be hostile creatures, natural hazards, or Solaria-driven phenomena. Unlike Obstacles, Dangers are active—they pursue, attack, or escalate if ignored. Facing a Danger always risks Morale, Supplies, or lives.
Anomalies Manifestations of Solaria’s corruption or blessing. Reality bends: shadows move against the sun, stone bleeds light, time falters. Anomalies embody the strangeness of the New World, and at least one in four Primary Hexes should contain them to reinforce its uncanny nature.

Secondary Hexes contain only the Biome and the Weather, and are otherwise empty of mechanical characteristics (unless the Archivist chooses to escalate them into Primary Hexes during play).

Hex Creation Procedure

e

Step-by-Step Generation and Seekers’ Influence

Use this loop whenever the party reveals a Primary Hex. The Archivist frames the Hex, generates its hidden characteristics, and presents signals in fiction; the Seekers respond by scouting, spending resources, or accepting hardship. The outcome fixes travel time, risks, and rewards for that Hex. Secondary Hexes skip most details unless the Archivist escalates them to Primary during play.

1

Set the
Frame

Archivist: Set the Frame

Choose the Biome to preserve geographic coherence. Note if this Hex is Primary or Secondary. If Secondary, decide whether to escalate to Primary due to story pressure, time, or player interest, otherwise skip to step 6.

↓

2

Assess
Modifiers

Archivist: Assess Modifiers

Check journey context: distance from last rest, injuries, burdens, prior Weather carrying over, and Luck availability. If the party has 0 Luck, the Archivist may use the Bad Luck column on generation tables.

↓

3

Generate Features

Archivist: Generate Hidden Features

Roll for Obstacles, Resources, Dangers, Anomalies, Special, and Weather. Keep results concealed unless a Skill/Mastery explicitly reveals them.

↓

4

Present
Signals

Archivist: Present Fictional Signals

Offer what is naturally perceivable at first glance or from a distance (e.g., storm on the horizon, river noise, smoke, forbidding silhouettes). These are clues, not full reveals.

↓

5

Influence
Results

Seekers: Influence Results

Before committing to the crossing, Seekers may spend Supplies, Morale, or Luck through Skills/Masteries to alter, reveal, or prepare for features. Costs are defined by each Skill/Mastery and may increase with journey length, harsh biomes, or current Weather.

↓

6

Finalize
Hex

Archivist: Finalize Hex and Lock In Results

Apply changes, lock in which characteristics are active or latent, and determine travel time (Primary: at least 1 day; Secondary: Archivist’s pacing). Note any triggers (e.g., “the Danger engages on camp,” “Obstacle tests happen at the ford,” “Anomaly pulses at noon”).

↓

7

Resolve
Passage

Archivist: Resolve Passage and Aftermath

Run any required tests/encounters, apply resource changes, Starvation/Despair if relevant, and check the Resource Flow rewards (e.g., accepting hardship → +1 Luck). Decide whether to lock the Hex (via Skills/Masteries/time) or leave it mutable for future resets.

Knowledge & Revelation

Archivist rolls and decisions remain hidden by default. Weather and obvious terrain are perceptible; everything else requires scouting, trial, or specific abilities. Some results may be latent (only trigger on camp/search), supporting tension without overloading the moment of entry.

Seekers’ Operations

When venturing into unknown territory, Seekers rely on their Exploration Skills and Masteries to shape the outcome of each Hex. The following are not direct actions to be chosen, but rather the common types of effects such abilities can produce. This overview is meant to illustrate how Seekers typically influence the landscape, threats, and opportunities of exploration.

  • Scout / Reveal: Learn one hidden characteristic (or narrow it) before committing to action.
  • Soften an Obstacle: Convert a hard barrier into a slower path or a one-use crossing.
  • Redirect a Danger: Delay, lure away, or change the trigger window of a threat.
  • Exploit a Resource: Improve yield or reduce the time/effort to harvest.
  • Distort Fate (Luck): Reroll, flip, or downgrade one negative result; cannot be used if Luck is 0.
  • Stabilize / Lockwork: Lay markers, maps, or sigils that help lock the Hex for future visits.

Game Example — Jungle River, Heavy Rain

The party leaves known trails and reveals a Primary Hex. The Archivist chooses Biome: Jungle and rolls in secret: Obstacle: flooded river with unstable banks; Resource: high-canopy fruit and clean rain catch; Danger: jaguar pair stalking the shore; Anomaly: shadows that drift counter to the sun; Special: obsidian waystone etched with solar glyphs; Weather: heavy rain.

At a glance the Seekers hear roaring water and see mist above the canopy—clear clues of a major crossing and bad weather. The Navigator spends Morale to Scout, revealing the river as the primary Obstacle. The Tinkerer expends Supplies to rig a rope-span, softening the crossing to a slower but safer ford. The party decides to accept the jaguars unchanged to conserve resources, immediately gaining +1 Luck (Accepting Hardship).

Crossing takes the baseline 1 day (Primary Hex), extended by rain to a grueling pace but without extra mishaps thanks to the rope-span. Near dusk the jaguars test the group—quick, tense exchange; the Seekers fend them off with minimal expenditure, earning +1 Morale (Triumph Over Danger). They harvest canopy fruit, replenishing Supplies. With daylight fading, they choose not to lock the Hex; on a future return, this area may reset under new rains, new tracks… or stranger shadows.

Section IV

d

Hex Travel Rules

d

Travel Time, Locking, and Resources

Hex Generation

Archivist rolls characteristics once; these determine dangers, anomalies, obstacles, structures, and Weather. No further rolls are needed.

Travel Time

  • Primary Hex: At least 1 day to cross; obstacles or harsh conditions may increase travel time or resource cost.
  • Secondary Hex: Time determined by Archivist.

Locking Hexes

The New World is mutable and unstable. Unless Seekers take deliberate actions to “lock” a Hex, it resets once abandoned.

  • Locking requires time, Skills, or Masteries (e.g., Cartography, Surveying).
  • Locked Hexes remain consistent on revisit.
  • Unlocked Hexes may shift features or biome.
  • Multiple Hexes can be locked if resources allow.

Supplies and Attrition

  • Without Supplies: Each day, every Seeker gains 1 Starvation token. Eating removes 1 token per day.
  • Without Morale: Each day, every Seeker gains 1 Despair token. Spending 1 Morale removes 1 Despair token from all.
  • Without Luck: Archivist may apply Bad Luck column for amplified negative effects.

Resource Recovery

Resources can be restored via rest, camp, or special items/abilities.

Section VII

g

The New World vs. the Seekers

g

Archivist Challenges and Seekers’ Choices

  • Archivist’s Results: Always generate challenges, scarcity, or obstacles.
  • Seekers’ Modifications: Favorable changes at the cost of shared resources.

This tension forces strategic decisions: spend resources to shape the environment or conserve them and face the threats as generated.

Section VIII

h

Maps and Progressive Discovery

h

Fog of War and Perception

  • Archivist’s Map (True Map): Complete and accurate; only Archivist has access.
  • Seekers’ Map (Perceived Map): Player-drawn, reflects limited perception, possible errors, or illusions.

Fog of War

Unvisited Hexes remain unknown. Players only see the current Hex and visible features at a distance.

Mismatched Maps

  • Generates tension and surprise.
  • Represents mutable and anomalous world.
  • Encourages strategic decision-making on trusting maps or local information.

Correcting the Map

Skills or Masteries like Cartography, Navigation, or Astronomy can reduce errors. Local maps, charts, or codices may reveal new Hexes or correct existing ones.

Section IX

i

Anomalies and Specials

i

Strange phenomena and unique discoveries that shape the New World.

Anomalies represent the influence of Solaria: reality twisted by solar energy, obsidian spires, unnatural storms, or living shadows. At least one hex in every four Primary Hexes should contain an Anomaly to maintain the sense of a world transformed by strange forces.

Specials are unique discoveries: ruins, sacred sites, indigenous settlements, or long-forgotten relics. Specials give texture and narrative hooks to the exploration.

Section X

j

Resource Flow: Risk and Reward

j

Balancing exploration risk with the party’s resilience and resources.

Exploration should never feel like pure attrition. The system rewards bold choices:

  • Accepting Hardship: If the Seekers accept a negative result without altering it, they gain +1 Luck (representing resilience and fate’s acknowledgment).
  • Resource Discovery: Finding Resources replenishes Supplies.
  • Triumph Over Danger: Surviving a Danger without major expenditure restores +1 Morale.

This cycle ensures the Seekers can continue deeper into the New World, but only if they balance risk and reward.
