What is a ttrpg?

b

Discover the world of tabletop roleplaying and the magic of shared storytelling.

Before we dive into rules, mechanics, or roles, it’s worth pausing for a moment to ask: What kind of game is this?

Seeker is a tabletop roleplaying game (TTRPG), a form of collaborative storytelling where players create fictional characters and guide their actions through imagined scenarios. Unlike board games, there are no winners or losers — the goal is to explore, to survive, to uncover mysteries, and to shape a shared narrative together.

At the heart of a TTRPG is the conversation between players and a special figure called the Game Master — or in Seeker, the Archivist. One person describes the world and its dangers, the others describe what their characters do. Dice are used to introduce uncertainty, tension, and dramatic turns of fate. Together, you breathe life into a forgotten chronicle!

This chapter will guide you through the core principles of play:

  • Who sits at the table and what roles they take
  • How scenes unfold and decisions are made
  • The different kinds of stories you can experience in Seeker

Whether you're a seasoned player or someone entirely new, this is where your journey begins.

Section II

Roles at the Table

j

Who does what: players, Archivists, and the dance of collaboration.

A session of Seeker typically involves one Archivist and two to five Seekers — the players. Each role has a different function in the shared experience.

The Archivist

The Archivist is the keeper of hidden truths. They describe the world, control its people and creatures, and present the challenges that shape the story. More than a narrator, the Archivist is a curator of the unknown — they don’t invent a fantasy, but reveal what has long been buried beneath lies and time.

In play, the Archivist:

  • Describes environments, events, and consequences
  • Controls all characters not played by the Seekers (known as NPCs)
  • Presents mysteries, dangers, and opportunities
  • Interprets the rules and guides the flow of the session

Rather than seeking to win, the Archivist’s role is to pose meaningful questions — and watch the players struggle, triumph, or fail in answering them.

Below are some guiding principles to help an Archivist play in a spirit that brings this history to life. These are not rules, but reminders — gentle nudges toward the kind of play that makes every session feel vivid, tense, and unforgettable.

DO

  • Present choices that matter — moral, strategic, or personal
  • Keep the mystery alive without withholding everything
  • Reward curiosity, caution, and boldness alike
  • Build tension through implication, not exposition
  • Ask “what do you do?” more often than you explain

DON'T

  • Script outcomes in advance
  • Punish players for missing “the right path”
  • Reveal all your secrets too soon
  • Get attached to your NPCs — let them fail, die, betray
  • Treat the rules as rigid laws when drama calls for flexibility

The Seekers

The other players each take on the role of a Seeker: an explorer, agent, or scholar tasked with uncovering the forbidden truths of the New World. Every Seeker has a distinct background, motivation, and set of skills that affect how they interact with the world.

In play, a Seeker:

  • Makes decisions for their character
  • Uses abilities and dice to overcome obstacles
  • Interprets clues and uncovers secrets
  • Contributes to the tone and pacing of the story

Though Seekers may come from different nations or Orders, they are united in purpose — and share the risks of the expedition.

Below are some guiding principles to help a Seeker play in a spirit that brings this history to life.

DO

  • Act as though your choices shape real consequences
  • Embrace uncertainty and incomplete information
  • Speak, plan, and argue in character
  • Use your abilities creatively — think like an explorer
  • Collaborate to keep the story moving

DON'T

  • Wait for the Archivist to hand you the “correct” answer
  • Solve everything with violencia (unless that’s your style)
  • Assume safety just because you’re the protagonist
  • Interrupt others’ moments in the spotlight
  • Be afraid to fail — failure reveals truth, too

How Play Works

a

The flow of adventure, decisions, and dice that bring Seeker to life.

In Seeker, gameplay unfolds as a conversation between the Archivist and the players. Each moment of the story is shaped through description, decision, and resolution.

Frame

The Archivist opens each moment of play by describing the scene.

Act

Players respond by declaring what their characters do.

Resolve

The Archivist applies the rules of the game and keeps the fiction moving forward.

1 • Frame

The Archivist Sets the Scene

The Archivist opens each moment of play by describing what the Seekers see, hear, and experience. This could be the flickering light in a ruined temple, the guttural cry of a distant beast, or the suspicious glance of a native envoy. Their narration provides a sense of place, mood, and immediate context.

The Archivist: “After hacking your way through thick vines, you emerge into a moss-covered clearing. At its center rises a crumbling temple of obsidian and bone. A strange, rhythmic thumping echoes from within — like a heartbeat carved into stone.”

2 • Act

The Seekers Declare their Intentions

Each player responds by declaring what their character does. This can be a bold action (“I draw my pistol and fire”), a cautious approach (“I examine the strange glyphs from a distance”), or even a question (“Can I tell if the creature is hostile?”). There is no fixed order — players speak when it makes sense for their character to act.

Isabella: “I signal the others to stop and crouch low. I want to observe the entrance silently — do I see movement or signs of recent passage?”

Mateo: “I step closer to examine the carvings on the outer wall. Are they similar to the ones we saw back in San Ignacio?”

3 • Resolve

The Archivist Determines the Outcome

Based on the action, the Archivist might call for a roll, describe a consequence, or introduce a complication. The Archivist interprets the results of the dice and keeps the fiction moving forward.

The Archivist: “Isabella, that sounds like a risky observation. Roll with Perception.”

[Isabella has a Perception score of 3. She rolls 3d6 Action Dice and gets one 6.]

[The Archivist rolls 1 Risk Die.]

The Archivist: “Isabella, you spot a fresh footprint in the mud — booted, not bare — and it’s not yours. Someone is inside. Mateo, the glyphs resemble those of the Silent Brotherhood — this temple may be part of the same network.”

Structured, but Flexible

While most scenes flow like a conversation, moments of danger or tension — such as combat, exploration, or social duels — follow a clear structure. Seeker uses turns, grids, and defined action rules whenever precision is required.

The game shifts smoothly between loose narrative and mechanical rigor, ensuring that both drama and clarity are preserved.

Types of Game Session

o

Different ways to explore the New World — from quick missions to epic campaigns.

When crafting a story for Seeker, three distinct types are commonly referenced. Each type defines the story’s scope, gameplay focus, and suitability for different kinds of players and groups. Understanding these categories helps Guides and players select the right kind of narrative experience for their needs and available time.

Scenarios

Scenarios feature a straightforward and focused narrative, often self-contained and resolved within a single or just a few sessions.

They typically emphasize one core aspect of gameplay—such as Combat, Social Interaction, or Exploration—making them ideal for players with limited time, newcomers wanting to learn the system, or those looking to explore specific facets of the game. Scenarios also serve well as spin-offs or side stories that complement larger arcs or chronicles.

Chronicles

Chronicles present a more developed and layered storyline compared to scenarios, weaving together multiple gameplay elements to create a richer narrative experience.

They usually span several sessions, allowing for character growth and plot complexity, making them well-suited for groups seeking ongoing adventures with deeper engagement in the world and its mechanics.

Arcs

Arcs represent complex, multi-session narratives with deep and evolving plots that require sustained player commitment and strategic engagement across various gameplay aspects.

These extended stories challenge players with intricate mysteries and long-term goals, providing a rich and immersive experience suited for groups ready to undertake ambitious and demanding campaigns.
